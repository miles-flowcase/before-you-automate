Chapter 1

The AI paradox
in proposals

Every major AEC firm is experimenting with AI right now. Proposal teams are piloting writing assistants. RFP automation platforms are racing to bolt on generative AI features. Conference agendas are packed with sessions on "AI-powered proposals."

And yet, for most firms, win rates haven't moved.

The proposals are getting assembled faster, sure. But faster assembly of mediocre content doesn't win work. Evaluators at DOTs, municipalities, and private clients aren't scoring your proposal on speed. They're scoring it on whether you proved your team can deliver.

AI is only as good as what it knows about your people. And right now, most firms can't even tell AI who their best people are.

This guide isn't about whether AI belongs in your proposal process. It does. This is about the step most firms are skipping — the one that determines whether AI actually helps you win, or just helps you lose faster.

If you're a Proposal Director, Marketing Director, or BD leader at an AEC firm and you've had the nagging feeling that something foundational is still missing from your AI strategy — keep reading. Some of your competitors have already figured this out.

Chapter 2

The real problem
isn't AI.

Let's be specific about what proposal teams are actually dealing with. Not in theory — in the day-to-day reality of assembling a response to a complex RFP with a two-week deadline and 50+ requirements across multiple roles.

The expertise data problem

Most AEC firms have incredible talent. Engineers with decades of experience on landmark projects. PMs who've delivered hundreds of millions in infrastructure. Specialists with certifications that took years to earn.

But that expertise isn't organized. It's not searchable. It's not structured in a way that any system — human or AI — can reliably access when it matters most.

Here's where it typically lives
Resumes in Word docs scattered across SharePoint folders and local drives
Project descriptions locked in old proposals that nobody can find
Certifications tracked in spreadsheets that haven't been updated in months
Skills and specializations known only through tribal knowledge
Bio information from acquired firms still sitting in their legacy systems

This isn't a technology failure. It's an infrastructure gap. And it's the single biggest reason AI proposals disappoint.

What this looks like at deadline

The scenario

Your firm is shortlisted for a $40M transportation project. The RFP requires 12 key personnel across three disciplines, each with specific certification and experience requirements. You have two weeks.

Your proposal manager starts the hunt. They email regional offices asking who's available. They dig through SharePoint hoping the CVs are current. They call project managers to verify whether someone actually worked on a comparable project.

They find three versions of the same person's resume — none of them updated in the last year.

Meanwhile, a competitor down the road runs a search, finds eight of their twelve people in minutes, and spends the remaining time tailoring their narrative to match every evaluation criterion.

Same talent pool. Same deadline.
Completely different outcome.
Chapter 3

3 reasons AI fails
in AEC proposals

The AI tools aren't broken. The inputs are. Here's what's actually going wrong:

01 — Unstructured data

Irrelevant recommendations

When resumes exist as static Word documents with inconsistent formatting, AI can't parse them reliably. It can't distinguish between someone who led a $200M highway reconstruction and someone who did surveying work on the same project. It treats all text as equal.

Result: Proposal managers still end up making every call manually.

02 — Stale data

Compliance risk you can't see

Certifications expire. People change roles. Firms acquire new companies. Yet the data feeding your proposal process often reflects the firm as it was 18 months ago — not today.

In regulated AEC work, submitting a proposal with an expired PE license can disqualify your entire submission.

03 — No governance

Hallucinated confidence

Without a governed system of record, there's no way to verify what AI produces. It might confidently assemble a team page featuring someone who left the firm six months ago, or attribute project experience that belongs to an entirely different office.

The scariest part isn't when AI gets it obviously wrong. It's when it gets it subtly wrong — and nobody catches it before the proposal goes out the door.

AI can't recommend the right people if it doesn't know who your best people are.

The principles that set them apart

WSB's story isn't unique — it's a pattern. The firms pulling ahead share a common playbook that has nothing to do with which AI tool they chose:

⬡

Treat expertise as infrastructure, not a document management problem.

◎

Invest in a system of record for people and project data.

▷

Standardize before you automate. Clean data first, AI second.

⊞

Make proposal data API-ready so future tools can access it.

⇌

Design for integration, not isolation. Work with HR, CRM, and PSA — don't replace them.

None of these principles require buying an AI writing tool. All of them make AI writing tools dramatically more effective when you're ready.

The AI readiness

Before you invest in any AI proposal tool, run through this checklist. If you can't check most of these boxes, you're not ready for Layer 2 — and any AI investment will underperform.

0 / 13

Check the boxes that apply to your firm.

Your People Data
Can you search for any employee by skill, certification, project type, or client — firm-wide?

Can you segment past projects by client, service line, region, and contract value?

Your Systems
Can AI tools access your expertise data through an API — without someone exporting a spreadsheet first?
